Image Based

AI Generated Content

Image-based AI-generated content of AIGC refers to the creation of images, videos, and visual media using artificial intelligence techniques. By training AI models on vast datasets of existing visual content, such as photographs, paintings, or videos, these models can learn patterns, styles, and structures present in the data. Once trained, they can generate new and original visual content that resembles the style or subject matter of the input data. This technology has been used to create realistic human faces, generate artwork, generate synthetic images for virtual reality, and even produce deepfake videos. However, it also raises ethical concerns regarding copyright, authenticity, and the potential misuse of AI-generated content for misinformation or malicious purposes.

Image Based in Industries

Image-based AI-generated content is transforming industries by enabling advanced applications such as educational visual aids, medical image analysis for diagnosis and treatment, personalized product recommendations, immersive virtual experiences, intelligent video surveillance, and facial recognition systems, revolutionizing the way we perceive, analyze, and interact with visual data.

Image-Based Feature

Image-based AI-generated content offers realistic visuals, enables style transfer, enhances efficiency and scalability, and allows for customization, revolutionizing industries and providing creative possibilities.

Realism: AI algorithms can produce visually realistic images that closely resemble real-world objects, scenes, or people, providing a high level of fidelity in the generated content.

Style Transfer: AI models can transfer the style or artistic characteristics of one image onto another, allowing for the creation of unique visual compositions or the replication of specific artistic styles.

Efficiency and Scalability: AI algorithms can generate content at a faster pace and in larger quantities compared to traditional manual methods, enabling streamlined productions and increased scalability.

Healthcare

The incorporation of Image-Based AI Generated Content (AIGC) in Healthcare is revolutionizing medical practice, ushering in a new era of precision, efficiency, and innovation. This technology employs machine learning and neural networks to analyze and manipulate visual data from medical images, leading to enhanced diagnosis, treatment planning, and patient monitoring. The AIGC tools not only augment physicians' capabilities but also contribute to early diagnosis, personalized treatment, and improved patient care. The vast availability of healthcare data significantly fuels the rising potential and applications of AI in medicine, signifying a paradigm shift that enhances medical decisions and patient outcomes.

Major Uses In Industry

Medical Imaging

Generative AI can synthesize medical images to augment datasets, which can then be used to train more robust diagnostic models. This could be particularly useful in situations where certain types of medical data are scarce or hard to collect.

Surgical Planning & Simulation

Generative models can be used to create 3D representations of a patient's anatomy for surgical planning or to simulate potential outcomes. This can help surgeons better prepare for procedures and explore various surgical options.

Anomaly Detection & Diagnosis

By learning the typical appearance of healthy tissue in medical images, generative AI can help identify anomalies, such as tumors or lesions, by recognizing when an image significantly deviates from the "normal" representation.

Features and Methods of AIGC application

Deep Learning:

Deep learning is a type of machine learning used in healthcare, specifically in medical imaging to improve diagnosis and treatment planning, such as in synthesizing CT from MR images.It utilizes features extracted from the hierarchical layers of the images, from simple to complex, through methods like Auto-encoder (AE), U-net, and Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs). These methods form a complexity hierarchy from basic AE, more complex U-net, to the advanced GANs, and are used to encode, decode, generate, and discriminate medical images for various applications in healthcare.

Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs):

Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) are a type of artificial deep neural network widely employed in image classification, segmentation, and recognition, functioning through complex layers that classify visual content, recognize objects, and cluster them. In healthcare, CNNs excel in medical image analysis, risk assessment, treatment planning, and drug discovery, demonstrating superior capability in detecting diseases on the MRI images and X-rays, often outperforming human accuracy.

Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs):

GANs can be used to generate synthetic medical images for research and training. It can augment a dataset for machine learning training when actual data is scarce or sensitive. In addition, GANs can simulate potential disease progression or model the potential effects of various treatment options based on current images.

Unsupervised Learning:

Unsupervised learning is a type of machine learning that uses generative models to understand the underlying distribution of unlabeled training data. This method, which includes techniques like cluster analysis and anomaly detection, is particularly beneficial in healthcare due to the frequent scarcity of labeled data, aiding in exploratory data analysis to discover hidden patterns or groupings.

This method, which includes techniques like cluster analysis and anomaly detection, is particularly beneficial in healthcare due to the frequent scarcity of labeled data, aiding in exploratory data analysis to discover hidden patterns or groupings.

Data Augmentation:

Generative AI can create synthetic data to augment existing datasets. This can be particularly useful in healthcare, where collecting large amounts of data can be difficult due to privacy concerns or the rarity of certain conditions.

Segmentation and Feature Extraction:

These methods are used to isolate and identify key features in images, such as tumors in a medical scan or signs of disease in a retina image. Convolutional neural networks are often used for this purpose.

Simulation and Prediction:

Generative models can be used to simulate potential outcomes, like how a patient might respond to a treatment or how a tumor might grow over time. This can help doctors make more informed decisions about treatment.

Advantages and Disadvantages

    Advantages
  • Deep learning methods enhance accuracy in tasks such as disease diagnosis, often outperforming traditional methods.
  • Process large volumes of data quickly, making them well-suited to the vast amounts of data.
  • Identifies patterns in data and make predictions about disease progression or patient outcomes, facilitating proactive healthcare interventions.
  • Provides valuable insights that improve patient diagnosis, treatment, and monitoring, leading to better patient care and outcomes.
    Disadvantages
  • The "black box" nature of deep learning models makes it difficult to understand how they arrive at a particular decision, which is a significant drawback in healthcare where explainability is crucial.
  • Data quality and quantity impact results, leading to biased or inaccurate medical outcomes.
  • The use of sensitive patient data raises concerns about data privacy and security, particularly given the susceptibility of AI systems to adversarial attacks.
  • Regulatory challenges arise from novelty, risks, and safety standards for deep learning

Challenges in Healthcare

Continuous learning and knowledge updates:

LLMs currently lack the ability to learn and update their knowledge base in real-time, posing a challenge in keeping up with the rapidly evolving medical evidence.

LLMs need to develop temporal reasoning skills to distinguish between current and outdated evidence, as medical guidelines and knowledge can change over time.

Inaccuracies and errors:

LLMs may miss important details, contain factual errors, incorrect data, or misrepresentations, leading to potentially unreliable information and answers.

Reasoning limitations:

Improvements are required in LLMs' reasoning abilities to enable complex, robust, and explainable thinking, ensuring more reliable and accurate outputs.

Reviewing lay summaries:

Lay summaries generated by LLMs may lack adequate accuracy and completeness, highlighting the need for expert review to ensure reliability and clarity for patients and the public.

Automated Grading

In today's fast-paced educational landscape, the integration of technology has revolutionized the way we approach learning and assessment. One significant advancement that holds immense promise is the utilization of computer vision in automated grading systems. Computer vision, a branch of artificial intelligence, empowers educators with the ability to streamline and enhance the grading process by automating the evaluation of handwritten assignments, tests, and even complex diagrams.


By harnessing the power of computer vision, educators can bid farewell to tedious hours of manual grading, allowing them to allocate their time and expertise towards more meaningful interactions with students. With sophisticated algorithms and pattern recognition capabilities, automated grading systems meticulously analyze handwritten submissions, accurately deciphering written text, mathematical equations, and graphical elements. This ensures consistent and objective evaluation, eliminating the potential for human bias and subjectivity.


Moreover, the benefits extend beyond efficiency. Automated grading systems provide prompt and detailed feedback to students, fostering a constructive learning environment. Students receive immediate insights into their strengths and weaknesses, enabling them to identify areas that require improvement and take corrective measures in a timely manner. This personalized feedback contributes to a more adaptive and tailored educational experience, empowering students to actively engage in their learning journey.


While acknowledging that automated grading systems cannot fully replace human evaluation, they serve as invaluable tools that complement and enhance traditional grading practices. Educators can focus on more intricate aspects of assessment, such as critical thinking, creativity, and problem-solving skills, while leaving the routine task of grading to the algorithms. This shift allows educators to dedicate their expertise towards fostering deeper learning experiences and providing guidance to students in areas that require individual attention.


One example of such application is Gradescope. Gradescope uses computer vision technology to automatically group scanned written submission together. This facilitates grading process as the same grading rubric will be applied to multiple submissons. Graders will not need to grsde similar sumission multiple times but they can do it in one single click. This creates a scalable system in large classes where TAs and Tutors will have to grade hundreds of Homework/ Exam submissions in a short period of time.

Entertainment

Image-Based AI Generated Content (AIGC), underpinned by advanced deep learning algorithms and neural networks, is revolutionizing the entertainment industry. This technology, which learns to analyze, interpret, and manipulate visual data from extensive datasets, enables the creation, alteration, and enhancement of images, videos, animations, and effects. Its integration is transforming the way media content is produced, distributed, and consumed, contributing to immersive storytelling, enriched virtual environments, and elevated user experiences. This substantial shift is facilitating a myriad of applications and ushering in a new era of creativity and innovation in the entertainment sector.

Major Uses In Industry

Content Creations

AI tools are capable of generating original images, videos, or altering existing ones. Special effects in movies or games are often created with AI to reduce both the time and cost associated.

Deepfakes & Synthetic Media

Hyper-realistic images and videos can be generated that are increasingly used in film and advertising, where a digital persona can substitute real actors or where older footage can be upgraded to higher resolutions or even 3D.

Object Detection and Localization

AI algorithms detect and localize objects in images, enabling object tracking, augmented reality, and automated image annotation. It enhances scene understanding and content organization.

Features and Methods of AIGC application

Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs):

GANs are a class of AI algorithms used in unsupervised machine learning, and they are currently the main driving force behind AIGC. They generate realistic synthetic data by training two neural networks simultaneously: one generates data, while the other evaluates it.

Style Transfer:

This feature allows AI to apply the style of one image to another - for instance, applying Van Gogh's brushwork style to a modern photograph. This has found a particular niche in the entertainment industry, especially in film and gaming.

Transfer Learning:

This method is widely used to apply knowledge gained from one AI model to another model. It helps in improving the accuracy and quality of the AI-generated content.

Content Manipulation:

AIGC enables content creators to manipulate images, videos, and animations, altering attributes such as lighting, colors, textures, and perspectives. It facilitates quick and efficient content iteration.

Procedural Generation:

AIGC can generate content procedurally, reducing the need for manual labor and speeding up content creation. This is particularly useful for generating large-scale environments or generating variations of assets.

Level of Required Human Input

The level of human input required in image-based AIGC varies depending on the application, use case, and platform. While some applications may require minimal human involvement, such as style transfer or procedural generation, others may necessitate extensive human supervision and creative input. Below is an overview on different levels of human inputs required for various types of applications, uses, and platforms:

Content Creation:

In the initial stages of content creation, human artists and designers play a vital role in providing artistic direction, conceptualizing ideas, and establishing the creative vision. They define the parameters and constraints for the AI algorithms to follow.

Training and Data Annotation:

Human inputs are required to curate and annotate training datasets. This involves labeling images, videos, and other visual assets with relevant tags, attributes, or semantic information to guide the AI algorithms during the training process.

Algorithm Development & Tuning:

Human experts collaborate with AI engineers and developers to design and fine-tune AI algorithms, ensuring they align with the specific requirements of the entertainment industry. This iterative process involves testing, refining, and validating the AI models based on artistic and technical standards.

Creative Control and Iteration:

Throughout the AIGC workflow, artists and content creators have the ability to provide feedback, make adjustments, and refine the AI-generated results. They bring their unique artistic sensibilities, making creative decisions and ensuring the output aligns with their vision.

Content Curation and Selection:

Human experts curate and select AI-generated content based on artistic vision and storytelling objectives. They ensure the output aligns with desired standards, filtering and choosing the most relevant visuals for entertainment projects. Their expertise adds a human touch, ensuring the selected AI-generated content resonates with the intended audience.

Quality Assurance:

Human oversight and quality assurance are crucial to maintain the artistic and technical integrity of the content. Human reviewers assess and validate the AI-generated results, identifying any issues or discrepancies, and making necessary adjustments to ensure the desired quality and consistency.
